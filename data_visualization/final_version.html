Effects of 9/11 on airports

The entire airspaces of the United States was closed ("ground stop") on 9/11 except for military, police, and medical flights. It was the first unplanned closure in the U.S. Domestic planes were diverted to the nearest available airport. All non-military flights needed specific approval from United States Air Force and FAA. There were only a few dozen private aircraft which received the approval in that time period. Civil Air Patrol's aerial photography unit was the earliest non military flight granted approval.

Many incoming international flights were diverted to Atlantic Canada to avoid proximity to potential targets in the U.S. and large cities in Canada. Some of the international flights that departed from South America were diverted to Mexico as well, however, its airspace was not shut down. On Thursday night, the New York area airports (JFK, LaGuardia, and Newark) were closed again and reopened the next morning. The only traffic from LaGuardia during the closure was a single C-9C government VIP jet, departing at approximately 5:15 p.m. on the 12th.

Civilian air traffic was allowed to resume on September 13, 2001, with stricter airport security checks, disallowing for example the box cutting knives that were used by the hijackers. First, the stranded planes were allowed to go to their intended destinations, then limited service resumed. The backlog of delayed passengers took several days to clear.

9/11 left a mark in these statistics. When comparing before with after the event, it is possible to notice that the number of flights cancelled were affected for a long period of time after the event.
